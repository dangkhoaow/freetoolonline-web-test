Microphone Test Online - What It Checks

Last reviewed 2026-04-27. Open the microphone test to check your mic in the browser. No upload, no install.

30-second answer. A browser microphone test confirms three things in under a minute: your selected mic is the one the browser is actually capturing, sound is reaching the input at a reasonable level, and what you hear back roughly matches what the other side will hear. It does not test microphone-to-meeting-app routing, system audio mixing, or hardware that your OS sees but the browser does not.

The three things the test confirms

  • Mic is selected. Phones, laptops, and desktops often have multiple input devices - the built-in mic, a USB headset, an HDMI capture device, a virtual loopback driver. The browser only captures from one. The test names which one is active so you can confirm or switch via the OS or browser audio settings.
  • Sound reaches the input. The level meter shows real-time amplitude. Speak at normal volume; the meter should jump to mid-range. A flat-line meter while you speak means the mic is muted (system or hardware mute switch), the wrong device is selected, or the cable is unplugged.
  • Playback matches input. Recording a short clip and playing it back tells you what the meeting will hear. The audio should be intelligible, free of crackling, and at a volume that does not require the listener to lean in or pull away.

What the test cannot check

Browser microphone capture is one path through the audio stack. Several real-world failures live outside that path:

  • Meeting-app routing. Zoom, Teams, and Google Meet have their own device pickers. Your browser test passing does not mean the meeting app is using the same mic. Inside the app, open audio settings and pick the same device.
  • System mixing and effects. Some operating systems and audio drivers add noise suppression, echo cancellation, and automatic gain control. The browser may receive raw input or processed input depending on the driver. The test cannot tell you which.
  • Hardware drift. A microphone that worked yesterday but produces noise today may have a failing capsule, a loose cable, or a USB hub power issue. The test surfaces the symptom; diagnosing the cause needs hardware swap testing.
  • Background noise filtering. Most meeting apps now filter background noise on the receiving side. Your test recording will sound noisier than what your colleague hears. That is expected and not a fault.

If the test fails

Three quick fixes catch most issues. Reload the browser tab and re-grant microphone permission. Try a different browser - Chrome and Firefox use different audio paths and one occasionally works when the other does not. Restart the operating system if a USB or Bluetooth mic worked earlier in the day but does not now. If all three fail, the device or driver needs attention.
