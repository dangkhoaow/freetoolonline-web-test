Two limits shape what a ZIP password unlocker can deliver, and naming them up front saves the most time. First, modern ZIP encryption is content-encryption, not container-marking: the bytes inside the archive are scrambled with a key derived from the password, and there is no shortcut from the ciphertext back to the plaintext without the key. Knowing the file names, the file count, or the archive's CRC does not help; those metadata fields are in the clear, but the file contents themselves are AES-256 (or for older archives, ZipCrypto) and only decrypt when the correct password is supplied. Second, "online" means uploading the archive somewhere; the realistic offline alternative for true forgotten-password cases is a desktop recovery product that runs dictionary attacks against likely-password lists on your own machine, which keeps the archive on your side of the network.

One pragmatic shortcut that often beats both of those: if the archive is your own and the source files still exist on disk, rebuild the archive with a fresh password at /zip-file-with-password.html rather than trying to crack the old one. You skip the cryptography problem entirely and end up with an archive whose password you choose and remember. If the archive was sent to you, ask the sender - that conversation usually completes in under five minutes and beats every online claim of automatic recovery.
