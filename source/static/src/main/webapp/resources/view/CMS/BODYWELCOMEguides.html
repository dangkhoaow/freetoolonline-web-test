Each entry in the index is one line: the title that names the reader task, and a short summary sentence that says who the guide is for. A reader who scans only the summaries can pick the correct walkthrough before clicking, instead of opening three pages and discovering the second one was the right answer. Topic blocks group the guides by job-to-be-done so the scan converges quickly on the input the reader actually has. The trade-off is that a reader who searches by tool name has to find the matching topic block first.

Every guide linked from this index ends in the same place: the bytes the reader works with - photos, archives, documents, hashes - never get uploaded to a server because the tools each guide drives run locally in the reader's browser. The hub does not repeat that contract per-guide. It states it once at the bottom so the privacy posture is the last thing a reader sees before clicking through to a walkthrough.

The index keeps Indonesian and Portuguese-language guide titles in their natural-language form rather than partitioning a separate locale hub. A reader who searches for kompres file zip or comprimir uma pasta lands on the same page as the English-language reader, finds their walkthrough alongside the English equivalents, and follows the same link out to a tool that runs in their browser. No country-specific routing, no second hub to maintain - one index for every reader who arrives.

Scroll past the last guide entry and the page closes with a single back-link to the homepage. The placement is deliberate: a reader who walked the entire index still has a one-click route to the site root without scrolling back to the top. The hub treats its bottom as a real reading endpoint, not a dead-end scroll - whichever guide the reader picked, the next step out of the index is one tap away.

The eight topic blocks on this hub are sorted by the input a reader is holding, not by alphabet or by tool name. ZIP and folder compression sit together at the top; HEIC and other image-format conversions follow; device-test guides for remote-work prep group around screen, camera, microphone, and keyboard checks; developer-utility guides collect hashes, minifiers, JSON, and regex; PDF guides group around merge, split, compress, and protect; video, GIF, and FFmpeg sit in one block; a behind-the-scenes block explains how the guides are written; and the closing privacy block names the shared contract. A reader who knows what file they have can jump to the matching block without scanning every entry.

The Behind-the-scenes block at the lower half of the index is built for readers who already finished the primary task and want context. Three kinds of article live there. Case-study pieces describe how the browser-only stack actually behaves at scale - the WASM cold-start tax, the privacy posture that retained users, the metrics that got measured wrong. Vs-articles compare two near-synonym tasks side by side (JPG vs JPEG, LCD vs LED, JSON vs YAML, MD5 vs SHA-256). Three-cause and four-fix troubleshooting pages list the specific failure modes the tools hit (camera test shows a black screen, microphone test reports no sound, GIF frame extractor output looks wrong) and the exact fixes a reader applies in order.

The way to use this index is the way a reader scans a magazine table of contents - look at the topic block titles first, jump to the block that names the input you have (a folder, a HEIC photo, a JSON payload, a webcam, a PDF, a video clip), read the per-entry summary line, and only then click through to the in-browser walkthrough. Developer-utility and PDF guides live in their own blocks so a reader holding a hash question, a JSON pretty-print task, or a merge / split / compress / password / flatten case lands on the right walkthrough without scanning the photo blocks, and the bilingual entries (kompres file zip, kompres zip, compactar pasta, comprimir uma pasta) sit next to their English siblings rather than behind a separate locale hub.
