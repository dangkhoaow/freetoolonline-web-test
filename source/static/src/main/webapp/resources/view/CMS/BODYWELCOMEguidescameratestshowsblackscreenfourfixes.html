A black browser-camera-test preview almost always means the device is in use, blocked, or shuttered - not faulty. The four steps work outward from the browser permission layer to the hardware shutter, in that order: the browser permission gate first, then the operating-system camera toggle, then any other process holding the single hardware stream, and finally the physical privacy shutter or BIOS-level disable. Most cases resolve in step 1 or step 3, which is why running the four steps in their named order saves the time you would otherwise spend tearing apart drivers or device-manager entries that were never the cause - the symptom of a black preview is rarely a faulty device, and the diagnostic path is the same shape regardless of which laptop or which browser is in front of you.

The first two layers each have a precise click path you can repeat in under a minute. Fix 1 is a browser permission reset that lives behind the lock-icon to the left of the address bar: open site settings, set Camera to Allow, then reload so the new permission takes effect. Fix 2 is the OS-level camera toggle: on macOS open Privacy and Security then Camera and toggle the browser on; on Windows open Settings then Privacy and Security then Camera and check that camera access plus desktop-app access are both on; on iOS Safari set Camera to Allow per-site; on Android Chrome the camera lives behind an app-level permission you can grant from app info. Most "stuck black" reports clear inside one of those two click paths.

Fix 3 is the one that catches most readers on a working laptop with a freshly reset permission. A webcam exposes a single hardware stream, so the first program to open the device wins and every later request sees a black frame. Close virtual-camera holders such as OBS Studio, Snap Camera, NVIDIA Broadcast, and Loopback, then quit any meeting client still running in the background - Zoom, Teams, Google Meet, Webex. The camera-indicator LED turns off the moment the holder releases the device.

Fix 4 is the physical privacy shutter on the lens bezel, or the BIOS-level webcam toggle on Lenovo and Dell business laptops; a black preview with no LED activity is the signature of a closed shutter, not a software fault.

The privacy shutter slides in front of the camera with a small switch on the top edge of the lid, so checking the shutter switch and the BIOS toggle belongs in the diagnostic before driver reinstalls. The BIOS-level webcam toggle on Lenovo, Dell, and HP business laptops lives under Security or Devices in the BIOS setup screen and overrides every later permission grant - the operating system never sees a device that the firmware has disabled. Both the shutter and the BIOS toggle produce the same black preview signature with no camera-indicator LED activity, so the closed-shutter shape is the read either way.

The reason a browser camera test ends on a black preview is almost always one of four causes ordered from the browser permission layer outward to the hardware shutter, and the four ordered fixes follow that same outward path: browser permission first, then operating-system camera toggle, then any other process holding the single hardware stream, and finally the physical privacy shutter or BIOS-level webcam disable. The order is not arbitrary. The browser permission gate is the closest cause to the page itself, and a denied or stale permission produces the same black preview as a held hardware stream or a closed shutter, which is why the cheap layers are checked first - a one-click permission reset clears the symptom in step 1 for most readers, and the rest never have to open Device Manager or reinstall drivers to find the actual cause.
